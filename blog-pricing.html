How to Price Your Product or Service as a Small Business Owner

Most small businesses underprice — not because they're generous, but because they don't know their real costs. Here's how to fix that with a practical pricing framework.

Pricing is one of the most consequential decisions a small business makes — and one of the least systematically approached. Most owners set prices based on what feels reasonable, what competitors charge, or what they think customers will pay. These are not bad starting points. But without a clear view of your actual costs, any price you set is essentially a guess.

The consequences of underpricing compound over time. You work harder, grow revenue, hire staff — and somehow the business never seems to generate the returns it should. The culprit is almost always margin: prices set without understanding the full cost structure, resulting in a business that looks successful and earns like one that isn't.

Start with costs, not competitors

Before you can price sensibly, you need to know what it actually costs to deliver your product or service. Not just the materials or the direct labour — the full cost, including a fair share of all your overhead.

True cost per unit — what to include

Direct costs — Materials, components, direct labour, packaging, shipping, merchant fees. These are the costs that exist because of this specific product or service.

Overhead allocation — A proportional share of your fixed costs: rent, salaries, software, insurance, marketing. These exist regardless of volume, but they still need to be covered by your pricing.

True cost per unit = Direct costs + (Total monthly overhead ÷ Total monthly units sold)

That last formula is the one most owners skip. They price based on direct costs and feel comfortable with their margin — but when overhead is properly allocated, the true margin is significantly lower. Sometimes it's negative.

The two most useful pricing methods

Method 1: Cost-plus pricing

Take your true cost per unit and apply your desired markup. If your true cost is $45 and you want a 60% markup, your selling price is $45 × 1.60 = $72. Simple and transparent.

Cost-plus pricing works well when your costs are well understood and consistent. Its limitation is that it doesn't account for what the market will bear — you might be leaving money on the table if customers would happily pay more, or pricing yourself out of the market if competitors are significantly cheaper.

Method 2: Target margin pricing

Decide on the gross margin you need and work backwards to the required selling price. If your direct cost is $30 and you need a 55% gross margin: Selling price = $30 ÷ (1 − 0.55) = $66.67.

This method anchors your pricing to a financial outcome rather than a cost multiple. It's particularly useful when you have a target margin that the business needs to hit to remain viable.

"The most useful thing about having a pricing model isn't the number it gives you. It's that it forces you to confront what your costs actually are."

Why overhead allocation changes everything

Here's a concrete example of how overhead allocation changes the picture. Suppose you run a small business with £8,000 per month in overhead costs and you sell 200 units per month. Your overhead per unit is £40.

If your direct cost per unit is £25, you might feel comfortable selling at £50 — that's a 50% gross margin on direct costs, which looks healthy. But when the £40 overhead allocation is included, your true cost is £65 and you're losing £15 on every unit you sell. The business looks profitable until it doesn't.

This isn't unusual. It's one of the most common patterns in businesses that are growing but not building wealth. More sales solves the revenue problem while leaving the margin problem completely intact.

When to price on value, not cost

Cost-based pricing has a natural ceiling: it tells you the minimum you need to charge, but not the maximum you could charge. Value-based pricing flips the question: what is this worth to the customer?

For professional services, consulting, software, and anything where the outcome matters more than the input, value-based pricing often results in significantly higher prices than cost-plus would suggest — and customers who are happy to pay them, because they're buying a result rather than an input.

When to use value-based pricing

Your offering saves the customer time, money, or risk · The outcome is clearly measurable · You have a differentiated product competitors don't offer · Your market is price-insensitive (premium, B2B, specialist) · The cost of the alternative (doing nothing or using a competitor) is high

Even if you ultimately price on value, you still need to know your cost-based floor. Value pricing without knowing your costs is just optimism. Value pricing with a clear cost model is a business strategy.

When and how to raise prices

Most small business owners wait too long to raise prices and worry too much about the response when they do. The research consistently shows that the volume impact of modest price increases is smaller than expected — particularly for businesses where quality, relationships, or expertise are the primary value drivers.

A 5% price increase on a business with 10% net margins improves profitability by 50% — assuming no volume loss at all. Even if you lose 5% of customers, the net effect on profit is positive. The maths almost always favour the price increase more than the gut feeling does.

When raising prices, a few things help: give existing customers advance notice, frame it around increased value or costs rather than just profit, and implement it for new customers first if you want to test the response before applying it across the board.

The quarterly pricing review

Pricing shouldn't be set once and forgotten. Costs change, markets shift, and the value you deliver evolves. A quarterly pricing review — going through each product or service line and asking whether the price still reflects the true cost and the value delivered — keeps your margins healthy without requiring a full strategic overhaul each time.

Look for three things in the review: any products where direct costs have increased without a corresponding price increase, any lines where the margin is below your target and has been for multiple quarters, and any products that are consistently selling at full price without pushback — a sign there may be room to increase.
